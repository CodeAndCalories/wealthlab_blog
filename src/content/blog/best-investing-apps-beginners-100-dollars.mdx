## What to Look for in a Beginner Investing App

Before we dive into the individual apps, it is important to understand what actually matters when choosing a platform. Not all investing apps are created equal, and the flashiest interface does not always mean the best experience for your money.

**Low or zero minimums** should be your first filter. If an app requires a $500 minimum deposit, it is not designed for someone starting with $100. Every app on this list allows you to begin with a small amount.

**Fee structure** is critical when you are working with limited capital. A $5 monthly fee on a $100 account is a 5% drag on your returns before the market even moves. Look for apps with percentage-based fees or no fees at all.

**Educational resources** matter more than most beginners realize. The best app in the world will not help you if you do not understand what you are buying or why. Look for platforms that teach as you invest.

**Fractional shares** allow you to buy a piece of expensive stocks like Amazon or Tesla without needing hundreds or thousands of dollars for a single share. This feature is essential for small-balance investors.

**Automation features** help you stay consistent, which is arguably the most important factor in long-term investing success. Round-ups, recurring deposits, and auto-rebalancing take the friction out of building wealth.

## 1. Acorns: Best for Completely Hands-Off Investing

If you want to invest without thinking about it, Acorns is the app that pioneered the concept of micro-investing. The premise is elegant: link your debit or credit card, and Acorns rounds up every purchase to the nearest dollar and invests the spare change into a diversified portfolio of ETFs.

Buy a coffee for $4.35, and Acorns automatically invests $0.65. It sounds small, but those round-ups compound over time in ways that genuinely surprise people. Many users report accumulating hundreds of dollars within their first year without ever making a conscious deposit.

Acorns offers five portfolio options ranging from conservative to aggressive, each built from a mix of stock and bond ETFs. You do not need to pick individual stocks or make any trading decisions. The app handles asset allocation and rebalancing automatically based on your risk tolerance and goals.

The pricing structure is straightforward. The Bronze plan costs $3 per month and includes the investment account, an IRA for retirement, and a checking account with a debit card that earns bonus round-up investments at select retailers. The Silver tier adds more features for families at $6 per month, and the Gold plan at $12 per month includes all features plus a premium educational library and live Q&A sessions with financial experts.

For a $100 starting balance, that $3 monthly fee represents 3% of your portfolio, which is significant. However, Acorns shines when you commit to consistent round-ups and recurring deposits. Once your balance grows past $500 or $1,000, that fee becomes far more reasonable as a percentage. The real value of Acorns is behavioral: it forces you to invest without relying on willpower.

[AFFILIATE: Acorns]

**Best for:** People who struggle to save consistently and want a completely automated experience.

**Starting minimum:** $0 (round-ups start investing immediately)

**Key drawback:** The flat monthly fee eats into small balances more aggressively than percentage-based pricing.

## 2. Betterment: Best for Goal-Based Investing

Betterment is a robo-advisor that takes a more sophisticated approach to automated investing. While Acorns focuses on simplicity and spare change, Betterment lets you set specific financial goals and builds customized portfolios to meet them. Want to save for a house down payment in five years? Betterment will construct a portfolio with the appropriate risk level for that timeline. Planning for retirement in 30 years? It will allocate more aggressively.

The platform invests your money in a diversified portfolio of low-cost ETFs, automatically rebalances your holdings, and performs tax-loss harvesting on taxable accounts. Tax-loss harvesting is a strategy where the app sells investments that have lost value to offset gains elsewhere in your portfolio, reducing your tax bill. This is a feature you typically only get from financial advisors who charge much higher fees.

Betterment charges 0.25% annually on your balance, which translates to just $0.25 per year on a $100 account. This percentage-based fee structure is significantly more favorable for small balances compared to flat-fee models. As your account grows, the fee scales proportionally rather than eating into a fixed dollar amount.

The educational content on Betterment is excellent. The platform explains every decision it makes with your money and provides articles, guides, and projections that help you understand concepts like compound interest, diversification, and risk tolerance. For a beginner, this transparency is invaluable.

Betterment also offers a high-yield cash reserve account, which can serve as a parking spot for your emergency fund while you invest separately for long-term goals. Having both options in one app simplifies your financial life considerably.

[AFFILIATE: Betterment]

**Best for:** Beginners who want automated investing with goal tracking and tax optimization.

**Starting minimum:** $0 (no minimum to open an account)

**Key drawback:** No individual stock picking if you eventually want more control.

## 3. Fidelity: Best for Zero-Fee Investing

Fidelity is one of the largest and most established brokerage firms in the world, and their mobile app has evolved into one of the best options for beginners who want to avoid fees entirely. Fidelity charges zero commissions on stock and ETF trades, has no account minimums, and offers fractional shares starting at $1. On top of that, they offer their own line of zero-expense-ratio index funds, meaning you pay absolutely nothing in ongoing fees.

Let that sink in: you can invest your $100 across a diversified set of index funds and pay zero in commissions, zero in account fees, and zero in fund expenses. No other platform on this list can match that cost structure.

The Fidelity app is more complex than Acorns or Betterment because it gives you more control. You can buy individual stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, and even options. For a beginner, this flexibility can feel overwhelming, but Fidelity addresses this with a robust learning center that includes articles, videos, webinars, and even a paper trading feature that lets you practice with fake money before risking real dollars.

Fidelity also offers retirement accounts including traditional and Roth IRAs with no minimums and no fees. If you are starting your investing journey, opening a Roth IRA at Fidelity and contributing even $25 or $50 per month can set you up for significant tax-free growth over decades.

The one area where Fidelity falls short for absolute beginners is automation. While you can set up recurring deposits, the platform does not offer the same kind of round-up or set-it-and-forget-it experience that Acorns and Betterment provide. You need to be somewhat intentional about logging in and making investment decisions, or at least setting up automatic purchases of specific funds.

**Best for:** Cost-conscious beginners who want maximum flexibility and zero fees.

**Starting minimum:** $0 (with fractional shares starting at $1)

**Key drawback:** Less hand-holding and automation compared to robo-advisors.

## 4. Robinhood: Best for Learning to Trade

Robinhood changed the investing world when it launched commission-free trading in 2015. While other platforms have since matched that pricing, Robinhood remains popular for its clean interface, fractional shares, and accessibility. You can start investing with as little as $1 and buy pieces of thousands of stocks and ETFs.

The app excels at making investing feel approachable. The design is intuitive, stock information is presented clearly, and the onboarding process is one of the fastest in the industry. You can go from downloading the app to making your first trade in under ten minutes.

Robinhood offers individual brokerage accounts, traditional and Roth IRAs with a 1% match on contributions, and a cash management account. The IRA match is noteworthy because it essentially gives you free money for retirement savings, something no other app on this list currently offers at the same level.

However, Robinhood comes with an important caveat for beginners: the app makes it very easy to trade frequently, and frequent trading is generally not a wealth-building strategy. Features like push notifications about price movements, real-time tickers, and easy access to options trading can encourage a short-term mindset that works against long-term compounding. If you use Robinhood, make a conscious decision to invest consistently and resist the urge to check your portfolio every hour.

Robinhood Gold is their premium tier at $5 per month, which includes higher interest rates on uninvested cash, professional research reports from Morningstar, and Level 2 market data. For a beginner with $100, Gold is unnecessary. Stick with the free tier until your portfolio and knowledge both grow significantly.

**Best for:** Beginners who want to learn about individual stocks and trading with a clean interface.

**Starting minimum:** $0 (fractional shares from $1)

**Key drawback:** The interface can encourage overtrading and short-term thinking.

## 5. SoFi Invest: Best for All-in-One Financial Management

SoFi started as a student loan refinancing company and has grown into a comprehensive financial platform. SoFi Invest is their brokerage arm, offering commission-free stock and ETF trading, automated investing through their robo-advisor, and even cryptocurrency trading, all within a single app.

What makes SoFi stand out for beginners is the integration with the broader SoFi ecosystem. You can manage your investments, banking, loans, and credit monitoring all in one place. This consolidated view of your finances is genuinely helpful when you are just starting out and trying to get a handle on your complete financial picture.

SoFi's automated investing option functions similarly to Betterment, building a diversified ETF portfolio based on your goals and risk tolerance. There are no management fees for automated investing, which is remarkable. You get robo-advisor service with zero advisory fees and no account minimums. For a $100 starting balance, this is hard to beat.

The active investing side lets you trade stocks, ETFs, and crypto with no commissions. SoFi also offers IPO access, letting you buy shares of newly public companies at the offering price, which is typically reserved for institutional investors or high-net-worth clients. While IPO investing carries significant risk, the access itself is a unique perk.

SoFi provides a steady stream of educational content through their app, blog, and social channels. They also host live events and Q&A sessions with financial planners, which adds a community element that can be motivating for new investors.

**Best for:** Beginners who want investing, banking, and financial management in one app.

**Starting minimum:** $0 (for both active and automated investing)

**Key drawback:** Crypto trading within the same app can tempt beginners into speculative positions.

## 6. Public: Best for Social and Educational Investing

Public takes a unique approach by combining investing with a social media-like experience. You can follow other investors, see what they are buying and selling, and join conversations about market trends and individual companies. Think of it as a blend between a brokerage and a financial social network.

The social element is a double-edged sword. On the positive side, it creates a community of learners who share insights and ask questions. Public has built a culture that leans more toward long-term investing than day trading, which is healthier for beginners. The platform also features live audio shows with financial experts and journalists who break down market events in real time.

Public offers commission-free trading with no account minimums and fractional shares starting at $1. They also provide access to alternative investments including fine art, collectibles, and royalties through partnerships with specialized platforms. While these alternative assets are interesting, beginners should focus on building a core portfolio of stocks and ETFs before venturing into exotic territory.

One of Public's best features for beginners is their transparency around payment for order flow. Unlike most free trading platforms, Public does not sell your trade orders to market makers. Instead, they let you add an optional tip to trades. This means there is no hidden cost embedded in your trade execution, and the company has been vocal about why this matters for retail investors.

The educational content on Public is woven into the social feed, making learning feel natural rather than like homework. Company profiles include analyst ratings, earnings data, and plain-language explanations of what the business does and how it makes money.

**Best for:** Beginners who learn best through community interaction and social features.

**Starting minimum:** $0 (fractional shares from $1)

**Key drawback:** Social features can create herd mentality around trending stocks.

## 7. Stash: Best for Thematic Investing

Stash takes a creative approach to making investing accessible by organizing investments into themes rather than ticker symbols. Instead of telling a beginner to buy VTI or SCHD, Stash groups stocks and ETFs into categories like "Clean and Green" for renewable energy, "American Innovators" for domestic tech companies, or "Moderate Mix" for balanced portfolios. This thematic approach helps beginners connect their investments to ideas and values they understand.

The app offers fractional shares, meaning your $100 can be spread across multiple themes and individual stocks. Stash also includes a banking feature called Stock-Back, which rewards debit card purchases with fractional shares of stock instead of traditional cash back. Buy coffee at Starbucks and receive a fraction of Starbucks stock. This feature is genuinely clever and makes investing a passive activity tied to your everyday spending.

Stash pricing starts at $3 per month for the Growth plan, which includes the investment account, banking, Stock-Back rewards, and access to financial education. The Stash+ plan at $9 per month adds custodial accounts for kids, enhanced Stock-Back rates, and monthly market insights reports.

Like Acorns, the flat monthly fee is the main concern for small balances. Three dollars per month on $100 is steep, but Stash compensates with the Stock-Back feature and strong automation tools. If you use the Stash debit card for regular purchases, the stock rewards can offset or even exceed the monthly fee.

The educational component of Stash is particularly strong. Every investment option includes a plain-language explanation, risk assessment, and historical context. The app is clearly designed for people who have never invested before and might be intimidated by financial jargon.

**Best for:** Beginners who want to invest according to themes and values they care about.

**Starting minimum:** $0 (with fractional shares available)

**Key drawback:** Monthly fee is high relative to small account balances.

## Head-to-Head Comparison

When you stack these apps against each other, the right choice depends entirely on what matters most to you.

If cost is your primary concern and you want the absolute lowest fees on a $100 balance, **Fidelity** and **SoFi Invest** are your best options. Both charge zero in commissions and advisory fees, and Fidelity's zero-expense-ratio funds are unmatched in the industry.

If you want a hands-off, automated experience, **Acorns** and **Betterment** lead the pack. Acorns wins on pure simplicity with its round-up model, while Betterment offers more sophisticated goal-based planning and tax optimization. Between the two, Betterment's percentage-based fee is far more favorable for a $100 balance than Acorns' flat $3 monthly charge.

If you want to learn about individual stocks and eventually develop your own investment thesis, **Robinhood** and **Public** give you the tools and community to do so. Public's social features and educational content make it the better learning environment, while Robinhood's IRA match is a compelling reason to use it for retirement savings.

If you want everything in one place, **SoFi Invest** integrates investing with banking, lending, and financial planning better than any other option.

## Tips for Investing Your First $100

Getting the right app is only half the battle. Here are some practical tips to make the most of your first $100 in the market.

**Start immediately, but invest consistently.** Your first $100 matters less than the habit of investing regularly. Set up automatic weekly or monthly deposits, even if they are just $10 or $25. Consistency beats timing every single time over long periods.

**Choose broad index funds over individual stocks.** When you are starting out, a total stock market index fund or an S&P 500 index fund gives you instant diversification across hundreds or thousands of companies. You can explore individual stocks later once you understand the fundamentals.

**Reinvest dividends.** Most apps let you automatically reinvest dividends, which means any cash payouts from your investments are used to buy more shares. This creates a compounding effect that accelerates your growth over time.

**Do not check your portfolio daily.** Markets go up and down every single day. If you are investing for the long term, daily price movements are noise. Check in monthly or quarterly, and focus on whether you are meeting your contribution goals rather than obsessing over short-term returns.

**Increase your contributions when your income grows.** The single most impactful thing you can do as a beginner investor is to increase the amount you invest over time. When you get a raise, a bonus, or a tax refund, direct a portion of it into your investment account before you adjust your lifestyle.
